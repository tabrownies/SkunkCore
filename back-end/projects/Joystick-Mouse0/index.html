Home Made Joystick

About

This joystick can be used as a mouse for any computer. It is built from raw materials: cardboard, two screw-on bottle caps, and old dowel, a bouncey ball, and some circuit boards. It's constuction too only required basic tools: scisors, soldering iron, and a hot glue gun.

I think the most impressive thing about the joystick is the melding of various, second-hand materials into the final product. For example, the dowel is succured by the cap to a chocolate milk jug. This give it free range of tilting motion while restricting translating motion. Similarly, the object attached to the dowel to trigger the buttons is the top of a water bottle. These bottle caps, as well as the cardboard, forced me to return to our families recycling bin frequently. Speaking of which, nothing but the circut boards are brand new. I reused many old broken things which were lying around the house to construct this joystick.

How it works

At the basic level, the joystick, right-click, and left-click are simply six individual buttons connected to a microcontroler, in this case an Arduino Yun. To ensure accurate readings of the buttons values a 270 ohm pull down resister is added to each button's circutry. After reading the current (or absence of) coming from each of the buttons, the Arduino microcontroler determines what commands to send to the computer to move the mouse. The mouse moving commands are all prepackaged with the Arduino software. These commands only work on a handful of Arduinos, hence the use of the Arduino Yun, despite the Yun's much grander abilities.

The buttons are positioned vertically around the dowel which makes up the joystick. As this dowel is moved back and forth, a bottle cap on the end presses these vertically oriented buttons. If the right button is pressed, the bottom of the dowel has been pushed right, meaning the top has been pushed left, and the microcontroler send the "Move Left" command to the computer. Similarly the other buttons are programmed to move the cursor oposite of the direcion they face.

How the Joystick and Enclosure were Built

The joystick is, at the core, an old dowel with a punctured bouncey ball stuck to the end for the user to hold. It is placed through a punctured milk carton cap. As mentioned above this cap allows the dowel to tilt, but not translate. Hot glue is added, which secures it in place. Below the milk lid, a water bottle lid is also attached via hot glue and a through-hole. This is a much more rigid connection and is what physically presses the buttons. The buttons are soldered to strips of perfboard, which are secured vertically by hot-glue in slits near the base of the joystick, positioned so that the bottle lid presses them. This is all glued to a thin piece of cardboard, which is in turn ducttaped to the bigger enclosure.

The strips of perfboard's wires are soldered into another perfboard containing the circuit, consisting of four identical cucuits shown below.

The left- and right-click buttons mirror the circut above, but electrically are not routed through the same perfboard. Although that would have been more efficient those buttons were more of an afterthought.

Going Forward

This build was inspired by a Lego Mindstorms creation I saw years ago on the now discontinued NXTLog. The creator had made a number of these joysticks, each iteration introducing upgrades from the last. Using what I remembered about his designed I constucted my joystick. Like him (or her), going forward I intend to continue to improve on the design.

  1. The next iteration will (hopefully) have a wooden container which will secure the joystick, buttons, microcontroler, and ciruitboards. It will be sturdier and use nicer buttons. As another feature it will also have a switch to activate different modes the joystick will be able to operate in.
  2. After that I will use a 3D printed enclosure which will allow me to add more buttons around the dowel for greater accuracey. With this iteration I intend to introduce a lot more features to the joytick. It will be pressable for a normal left click and also spin for scrolling.
  3. The final verson will have analog sensors to gain extremely accurate measurements of the joystick's tilt.
